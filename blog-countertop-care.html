How to Keep Your Stone Countertops Looking New for Decades

February 10, 2025 • 3 min read • By Steamboat Masters Granite

One of the biggest advantages of natural and engineered stone countertops is their longevity. With proper care, your surfaces will look as stunning in twenty years as they did on installation day. Here is what we teach every client during our final walkthrough.

Daily Care — All Materials

The daily routine is simple and identical for granite, quartz, and quartzite: warm water and mild dish soap with a soft cloth. That is it. Wipe down your surfaces after cooking, clean up spills as they happen, and dry the surface if it has been exposed to standing water.

Avoid harsh chemicals, bleach, oven cleaners, and abrasive pads on any stone surface. These are unnecessary and can damage the finish or sealant over time. A pH-neutral stone cleaner is an excellent alternative to dish soap if you want a dedicated product.

Quartz — The Zero-Maintenance Surface

If you chose quartz, congratulations — your maintenance routine is essentially done. Quartz is non-porous and does not require sealing, polishing, or any special treatment. The daily wipe-down described above is all it needs.

The one thing to watch with quartz is heat. Always use trivets for hot pans. While quartz handles moderate heat, prolonged contact with extreme heat can damage the resin binders and cause discoloration.

Granite and Quartzite — Simple Annual Sealing

Granite and quartzite are natural stones with microscopic pores. We professionally seal every surface during installation, creating a protective barrier against stains and moisture. This sealant lasts roughly 12 months under normal kitchen use.

Resealing is a straightforward process that takes about 15 minutes. Apply a quality stone sealant with a soft cloth, let it absorb for the recommended time (usually 15 to 20 minutes), then buff off the excess. We teach every client exactly how to do this during installation, and we are always available if you have questions.

The quick test: drop a few drops of water on the surface. If the water beads up, your seal is still strong. If it begins to darken the stone, it is time to reseal.

Bathroom Surfaces

Bathroom countertops and shower surrounds follow the same basic principles. Clean with a soft cloth and mild cleaner, avoid acidic or harsh bathroom chemicals directly on the stone, and do not let water stand around faucets for extended periods.

In bathrooms, mineral buildup from hard water is the main concern. Wiping faucet areas dry after use prevents calcium deposits from forming. If buildup does occur, a gentle stone-safe calcium remover will handle it.

What to Avoid

Never use vinegar, lemon juice, or acidic cleaners on natural stone. Acids can etch the surface of granite, marble, and quartzite, creating dull spots that require professional repolishing.

Never use abrasive pads like steel wool or rough scrubbing sponges. Soft cloths and microfiber towels are all you need.

Never sit or stand on countertops. While stone is incredibly strong, it does not flex. Concentrated weight on unsupported areas near sinks or edges can cause cracking.
